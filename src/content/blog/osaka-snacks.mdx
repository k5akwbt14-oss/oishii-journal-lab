Osaka's civic motto is *kuidaore* — roughly, "eat yourself into ruin" — and the city means it. This is Japan's street-food capital: takoyaki (octopus dumplings) sizzling on every corner, neon crab signs, and a general agreement that dinner should be loud.

Here's the part nobody tells you: a surprising share of the Japanese snack aisle is secretly Osakan. This stop in our regional series covers five things you can order in the US — including two icons you may not have known were from Osaka at all.

## 1. Takoyaki-flavor snacks: the sauce is the point

You can't ship a fresh takoyaki ball, but Japan has spent decades converting the *flavor* into shelf-stable form: puffed corn sticks, rice crackers, and chips all wearing that unmistakable sauce — sweet, tangy, Worcestershire-adjacent — with a wave of bonito and seaweed on top.

The octopus itself is mostly a rumor in these snacks. What you're really buying is Osaka's sauce culture, and the sauce carries the whole show. Start with the puffed-stick versions; they hold the most flavor dust per bite.

## 2. Pocky: yes, Pocky is from Osaka

Pocky's maker, Glico, has been an Osaka company for a century — its giant neon runner sign over the Dotonbori canal is the single most photographed advertisement in Japan. The world's favorite chocolate biscuit stick is, by passport, Osakan.

You know Pocky. What's worth knowing is the range: almond crush, matcha, the winter-only thicker cuts. It remains the most sharable snack Japan ever designed — the box is engineered for handing to the person next to you.

## 3. Pretz: the savory sibling

Same maker, same biscuit-stick idea, zero chocolate. Pretz goes savory instead: salad, tomato, roast, butter. It's the one you eat absentmindedly through a whole movie, and the flavors skew noticeably more "Japanese grocery" than export-line Pocky.

If you're building a care package, the unwritten rule is one sweet stick, one savory stick. Pretz is the savory stick.

## 4. Cream Collon: the third Glico on this list

Little rolled waffle cylinders with a cool vanilla cream inside — crunchy shell, soft center, gone in two bites. Cream Collon has been a Japanese childhood staple for decades and travels well, which makes it a reliable "something soft in a box of crunchy things."

And yes: Glico again. We told you Osaka runs the snack aisle.

## 5. Chicken Ramen: instant noodles started in an Osaka shed

In 1958, in a backyard workshop in Ikeda, Osaka, Momofuku Ando invented instant ramen — and his first product, Nissin's Chicken Ramen, is still on shelves essentially unchanged. There's a museum on the spot now. Every cup of instant noodles on Earth traces back to that shed.

Is it a snack? In Japan, honestly, yes — students crumble it straight over rice or eat it dry. The noodle block has the seasoning baked in: no packet, three minutes, weirdly perfect.

## The ones that stay in Osaka

<Callout title="Honesty corner">
Osaka's true heavyweights don't ship. **551 Horai's pork buns** are the item every traveler carries onto the bullet train home — fresh only. **Rikuro's jiggly cheesecake** wobbles for a day and then it's over. **Baton d'or**, Glico's department-store luxury Pocky, mostly refuses to leave Kansai. And takoyaki itself needs the griddle, the crowd, and the risk of burning your mouth. Plan a trip; bring stretchy pants.
</Callout>
