## 1. The matcha Kit Kat: where the flavor craze started

Japan's flavored Kit Kats are famous, but matcha is the one that conquered the world — and the serious versions name their source: **Uji**, Kyoto's tea country. Nestlé Japan even makes editions with Kyoto tea houses whose names go on the box, a co-sign Japanese shoppers actually check for.

A good matcha Kit Kat is not a green candy bar. It leads with the tea — a little grassy, a little bitter — and lets the white chocolate do the sweetening. If you've only had matcha in latte form, this is the cheapest possible field trip.

## 2. Yatsuhashi: cinnamon, the Kyoto way

Kyoto's signature souvenir is *yatsuhashi* — a thin rice-flour sweet flavored with *nikki*, Japanese cinnamon. It comes two ways. The baked version is a crisp, curved cookie that keeps for weeks and ships fine. The soft, unbaked version (*nama yatsuhashi*, folded around red-bean paste) is what every school trip to Kyoto brings home — but it only lasts days, so it mostly stays in Japan.

The cinnamon here is cooler and sharper than the American cinnamon-roll kind. It tastes old-fashioned in the best sense, like something served with tea in a room with paper windows — which is exactly what it is.

## 3. Konpeito: tiny sugar stars from the old capital

*Konpeito* are small star-shaped sugar candies, introduced by Portuguese traders in the 1500s and adopted so thoroughly that Kyoto still has shops that make almost nothing else. Each batch tumbles in a heated pan for days while syrup builds up the spikes — a genuinely absurd amount of craft for a candy the size of a pea.

The taste is simple sugar with light fruit or tea flavors; the point is the sparkle. They're the standard "little gift from Japan," and a jar on a desk disappears faster than anyone admits.

## 4. Uji matcha powder: the real thing

At some point the matcha Kit Kats send you up the mountain: actual Uji matcha, whisked into hot water or dumped honestly into a latte. Kyoto's tea merchants — some centuries old, a few with US storefronts now — sell tins of the same powder used in tea ceremony.

Two words to know. **Culinary grade** is for lattes, baking, and ice cream: cheaper, more bitter, made to fight through milk and sugar. **Ceremonial grade** is for drinking straight. Start culinary; graduate if it becomes a habit. It will.

## 5. Hojicha sweets: the toasty sibling

*Hojicha* is green tea roasted until it turns amber — the bitterness drops out and a toasty, caramel-adjacent flavor takes over. Kyoto shops popularized it, and it's quietly becoming the next matcha: hojicha lattes, hojicha chocolate, hojicha candy.

If matcha reads as too grassy for you, hojicha is the on-ramp. It pairs with milk the way coffee does, minus most of the caffeine punch.

## The ones that stay in Kyoto

<Callout title="Honesty corner">
Kyoto's finest rarely make the crossing. **Cha no Ka** — a matcha *langue de chat* cookie (thin French-style butter cookie) from Malebranche — is arguably Japan's best matcha sweet and is stubbornly hard to find stateside. **Nama yatsuhashi** is too fresh to ship, and **abura mochi**, a grilled rice cake sold outside one shrine by one very old shop, requires you to physically stand in Kyoto. Reasons to visit, not flaws in the plan.
</Callout>

## Buying matcha from the US: read this first

<Callout title="Buy smart">
- **"Uji" on the label is the quality shortcut.** Generic "green tea flavor" and Uji matcha are different weight classes.
- **Kit Kats are chocolate** — in summer heat waves, check listings for insulated shipping, or start with yatsuhashi and konpeito now. Our [melt-proof summer list](/blog/japanese-summer-snacks/) has the full workaround.
- **Import listings run above Japanese store prices** — often 2–4×. Check pack counts before you click buy.
