This stop in our regional series covers four Kyushu classics you can order in the US — including one you may have been eating since childhood without knowing where it was from.

## 1. Castella: the sponge cake that sailed in

*Castella* is a tall, springy honey sponge cake with a dark caramelized top and bottom, brought to Nagasaki by Portuguese traders in the 1600s and perfected there ever since — the oldest shops have been baking it for literal centuries. It's barely a "snack": it's the cake Japanese families serve when someone visits.

The texture is the event — denser than Western sponge, moist enough to need no frosting, with a faint crunch of coarse sugar along the bottom. With black coffee it disappears at an alarming rate.

## 2. Mentaiko snacks: the spicy flavor Japan swears by

Fukuoka's signature ingredient is *mentaiko* — pollock roe cured with chili — and Kyushu puts the flavor on everything: rice crackers, puffed corn sticks, chips, baguette spreads. It's savory, faintly briny, and warm rather than punishing; Japan's idea of "spicy" is a slow handshake, not a dare.

The fresh stuff needs a refrigerator, but the snack versions ship fine and are the honest gateway. If you see *mentai* on a Japanese snack in a US store, that's Kyushu talking.

## 3. Botan Rice Candy: the one you already know

If you grew up near an Asian grocery in America, you know the little red-and-green box: soft citrus candy wrapped in rice paper that melts on your tongue, plus a sticker inside. Here's the reveal — it's from Kagoshima, at Kyushu's southern tip, where Seika Foods has made its ancestor, *Bontan Ame*, for about a century. The flavor comes from the *bontan*, a huge mild citrus grown in the region.

That edible wrapper has been quietly blowing American kids' minds since the 1960s. It remains the cheapest time machine on this site.

## 4. Tonkotsu ramen kits: Fukuoka in a box

*Tonkotsu* — the rich, cloudy pork-bone broth — is Fukuoka's gift to the world, and boxed versions from Kyushu's famous shops have become the region's blockbuster souvenir. The kits from Ichiran, Fukuoka's cult ramen chain, are so popular that travelers budget suitcase space for them, and importers now list them in the US at a suitably dramatic markup.

A meal more than a snack, yes — but no honest Kyushu list can skip it, and a kit plus a soft-boiled egg is the best fifteen-dollar Friday night in this series.

## The ones that stay on the island

<Callout title="Honesty corner">
Kyushu keeps its deepest cuts local. **Black Montblanc** — a vanilla ice cream bar in crunchy chocolate that every Kyushu kid was raised on — obviously can't cross an ocean. **Hiyoko**, the chick-shaped bean-paste bun, mostly travels only as far as Tokyo (where, to Fukuoka's eternal irritation, people assume it's a Tokyo sweet). And **ikinari dango**, Kumamoto's steamed sweet-potato dumpling, is strictly a make-it-fresh situation.
</Callout>

## Ordering from the South: read this first

<Callout title="Buy smart">
- **"Mentaiko flavor" ships; real mentaiko is a refrigerated product** — as a beginner, buy the snacks, not the jar.
- **Castella squashes.** Look for boxed listings from established bakeries rather than loose slices, and expect the import premium.
- **Import listings run above Japanese store prices** — often 2–4×; ramen kits are the most marked-up item in this article. Check pack counts before you click buy.
</Callout>
