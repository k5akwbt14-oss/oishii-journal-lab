## 1. Chinsuko: shortbread from a royal court

*Chinsuko* is Okinawa's signature cookie: a small, crumbly shortbread that dates back to the islands' royal-court era, when it was a delicacy for nobility. The traditional recipe uses lard instead of butter, which sounds alarming and tastes wonderful — lighter and sandier than Scottish shortbread, with a clean snap.

The modern hit is the **sea-salt version** (*yuki shio*, "snow salt," milled from local seawater), where the salt sharpens the sweetness. Boxes of mixed flavors — plain, salt, brown sugar, purple sweet potato — are the standard Okinawa souvenir, and they ship without drama.

## 2. Kokuto: brown sugar you eat like candy

Okinawa grows sugarcane, and *kokuto* — black sugar — is the island's unrefined answer to candy: pressed cane juice boiled down and cut into rough chunks, keeping all the minerals refining strips out. The flavor goes far past sweet, into molasses, coffee, and something almost smoky.

Islanders eat it straight, a chunk with hot tea. Each island's kokuto tastes slightly different, and Okinawans discuss this the way the French discuss wine. Start anywhere; the candies and coated peanuts count too.

## 3. Beni-imo sweets: purple on purpose

*Beni-imo* is Okinawa's purple sweet potato, and the color is real — that saturated violet is the vegetable, not food dye. The flagship product is the **beni-imo tart**: a little boat-shaped shortcrust filled with vivid purple sweet-potato paste, sold in boxes that move through Okinawa's airport by the pallet.

The taste is gentle — sweet potato with a vanilla softness, less sugary than the color implies. Beyond the tarts, the flavor shows up in Kit Kats, chips, and cookies; it photographs absurdly well and tastes like a vacation.

## 4. Shikuwasa candy: the tiny green citrus

*Shikuwasa* is Okinawa's native citrus — golf-ball sized, green, and aggressively tart, somewhere between a lime and a very opinionated tangerine. Fresh ones rarely leave the islands, but the flavor travels as hard candy, gummies, and bottled juice you dilute like cordial.

It's the palate cleanser of this list: sharp, cold-tasting, the thing you want after too much kokuto. Okinawans also swear by it in drinks, on grilled fish, and in roughly everything else.

## 5. Sata andagi: the islands' doughnut

*Sata andagi* are Okinawan doughnuts — dense, round, crackle-topped fritters, crunchy outside and cakey inside, traditionally fried for celebrations. Fresh ones don't ship (day-two sata andagi is already a lesser sata andagi), but Okinawan mills sell the **mix**, and the recipe is genuinely forgiving: shape, fry, done.

It's the one item on this list that comes with a kitchen project attached, and the payoff-to-effort ratio is excellent. Brown-sugar versions of the mix bring item #2 back into the story.
