Hokkaido is Japan's northern island: dairy farms, snowfields, and — as a direct result — the snacks the rest of Japan is most jealous of. Milk, butter, and cream from Hokkaido are a quality claim on any package sold anywhere in the country. When Japanese travelers fly home from Sapporo, half the carry-ons hold the same handful of boxes.

## 1. Shiroi Koibito: the white-chocolate legend

Two paper-thin *langue de chat* cookies (crisp French-style butter cookies) with a sheet of white chocolate pressed between them. That's the whole product. Ishiya has made it in Sapporo since 1976, and it has been Japan's default souvenir gift roughly ever since — there is an entire theme park in Sapporo dedicated to this one cookie.

The name means "white sweetheart," after the first snowfall of the year. The cookie snaps clean in half, the white chocolate is milky rather than sugary, and one is never enough. If you buy a single item from this list, you can't go wrong here.

There's also a milk-chocolate version in a black box — the locals' pick, quietly.

## 2. Royce' Nama Chocolate: the one that needs a fridge

*Nama* means "fresh" — and Royce' means it literally. These are soft squares of ganache made with Hokkaido cream, dusted in cocoa powder, sold from a refrigerator case and shipped with cold packs. The texture is the point: they don't snap, they dissolve.

Royce' is a Sapporo chocolate maker with a real US presence, so this is the rare regional item with an official cold-chain route across the Pacific. Au Lait (milk chocolate) is the classic first box.

## 3. Royce' Potato Chip Chocolate

Royce's other famous move: ridged salted potato chips, half-coated in milk chocolate. Sweet-salty done with lab precision — the salt is doing real work, not decoration. Japan considers this a completely reasonable thing to make, and after one chip you will too.

It travels far better than nama chocolate, though it's still chocolate — see the summer note below before ordering in a heat wave.

## 4. Jaga Pokkuru: the potato sticks with a cult

Calbee's Hokkaido-only snack: skin-on Hokkaido potatoes cut like tiny french fries, lightly salted, impossibly crisp. For years the running joke was that Jaga Pokkuru sold out at the Sapporo airport before lunch. The joke was accurate.

Amazon searches will also surface **Jagabee** — the nationwide sibling sold in cups. Same maker, very close taste, easier to find and cheaper. Purists hunt the Hokkaido original; hungry people buy whichever is in stock.

## 5. Yubari melon candy: a $45,000 melon for $3

Yubari King melons are Hokkaido's luxury fruit — perfectly round cantaloupes from the town of Yubari, so prized that the first pair auctioned each season has sold for more than a new car. Nobody snacks on a melon like that.

Candy makers solved it. Yubari melon gummies, hard candies, and caramels deliver that floral, honeyed melon flavor for pocket change, and "Yubari melon" on a Japanese package signals real flavor ambition, not generic "melon." It's the most affordable brush with luxury on this site.

## The ones that stay on the island

<Callout title="Honesty corner">
Hokkaido's deepest cuts don't ship. **Marusei Butter Sand** (Rokkatei's butter-cream, rum-raisin, and biscuit sandwich) has a short shelf life and rarely survives export channels, and the island's famous soft-serve obviously stays home. If a trip to Sapporo is ever on your calendar, those two go at the top of the eating itinerary — with a fridge-case run at the airport on the way out.
</Callout>

## Ordering chocolate in July: read this first

<Callout title="Buy smart">
- **Three of these five are chocolate.** In hot months, look for listings that mention cold packs or insulated shipping — or order the chocolate items in fall and start with Jaga Pokkuru and melon candy now. Our [melt-proof summer list](/blog/japanese-summer-snacks/) exists for exactly this reason.
- **Import listings run above Japanese store prices** — often 2–4×. Check pack counts and per-item prices before you click buy.
- **Shiroi Koibito counts differ** (12, 18, 24, 36 per box). The cookie is identical; only the math changes.
</Callout>
